A data story · Internet Access 2022

Connected & Left Behind

In 2022, Saudi Arabia achieved 100% internet penetration. Eritrea was at 9%. Between those two numbers lies one of the starkest divides in modern life — and a story that looks very different depending on which continent you call home.

Scroll

Think about the last time you looked something up, sent a message, paid a bill, or applied for a job. Nearly everything that operates a modern life requires the internet. Yet as of 2022, nearly a billion people worldwide still had no access to it — and the gap between those who do and those who don't has become one of the defining inequalities of the century.

The original Tableau dashboard this story is based on shows all 193 countries at once, ranked by internet penetration rate. It's comprehensive. It's thorough. And it's almost impossible to read — a wall of bars that, if you stare at it long enough, starts to blur into a general sense of "some places have more than others."

But the data has a story inside it, and it's actually several stories. Not one trend, but a continent-by-continent divergence that reveals how geography, wealth, infrastructure investment — and sheer luck of where you were born — still determine who gets to participate in the modern economy.

The chart above tells a clear story at the regional level. Europe and North America sit comfortably above 85%. East Asia and the Pacific have made enormous strides, propelled by South Korea, Singapore, Australia, and Brunei. The Middle East shows surprising strength, thanks largely to Gulf states that have poured oil wealth into digital infrastructure.

Africa's 54 countries, divided

Sub-Saharan Africa is not one place. Lumping it into a single regional average obscures the fact that within the continent, there's a near-complete spectrum — from South Africa at 72% to Eritrea at 9%. The countries clustered in the bottom 20 globally are almost all here.

What separates them? Telecommunications infrastructure investment, political stability, GDP per capita, and geography all play roles. Landlocked countries without undersea fiber cable access pay far more per megabit than coastal nations. That price difference alone is enough to lock millions out.

The spread here is striking. Rwanda at 36%, Kenya at 40%, Ghana at 54%, South Africa at 72% — these are countries investing heavily in fiber and mobile broadband. But then there's a long tail: Chad at 21%, Central African Republic at 15%, Eritrea at 9%.

Many of the lowest-ranked countries are also among the world's most conflict-affected or economically marginalized. The digital divide doesn't exist independently of other inequalities — it mirrors them almost exactly.

The top of the world is more surprising than you'd think

At the other end of the spectrum, the story is equally interesting — but for different reasons. The highest internet penetration rates in the world don't all belong to rich Northern European democracies. Several Gulf states — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain — report 100% penetration. Kuwait is at nearly 100%.

These are nations that have built digital infrastructure as a deliberate policy project, partly to diversify economies away from oil, and partly as a function of small, urban, wealthy populations. The lesson: high internet access isn't just about democracy or press freedom. It's about investment priority and population density.

Notice that every single country in the bottom 10 is in Sub-Saharan Africa. And the top 10 is a genuinely surprising mix: Gulf states sit alongside Denmark, Luxembourg, Monaco, Liechtenstein, and Brunei Darussalam — not the usual roster you'd expect from a "who's winning at the internet" list.

This matters because the narrative of "rich Western countries lead, poor Global South lags" is only partly true. It ignores the Gulf's massive infrastructure investment. It ignores East Asia's rapid rise. And it ignores the fact that within Africa, there are countries making real progress — even as others remain almost entirely offline.

The internet was once described as the great equalizer — a technology that would flatten hierarchies and give everyone access to the same information and opportunity. The data from 2022 tells a more complicated story. For hundreds of millions of people, the great equalizer is still out of reach, not by choice, but by where they happened to be born.

Whether that changes in the next twenty years will depend less on the technology — which exists, and is improving — and more on the political will to make access a right rather than a luxury.
